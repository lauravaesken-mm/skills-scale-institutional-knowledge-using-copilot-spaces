# OctoAcme Project Management Docs

Welcome! This README provides an overview and quick navigation to all OctoAcme's project management process documentation.

## Summary of OctoAcme Project Management Processes

OctoAcme follows a structured, iterative project management lifecycle consisting of five key stages: Initiation, Planning, Execution, Release, and Closing/Retrospective. Grounded in principles of customer-first value and clear ownership, projects begin with a "Project One-pager" to validate business needs and move into planning once success metrics and stakeholder alignment are confirmed. This lifecycle ensures that every initiative is backed by a clear problem statement and measurable goals before resources are committed.

The framework defines three core roles: **Project Managers (PM)**, who coordinate delivery and risk; **Product Managers (PdM)**, who own the vision and prioritize the backlog; and **Developers**, who implement features and maintain code quality. Communication is handled through a tiered cadence, including twice-weekly team standups, weekly syncs between the PM and PdM, and monthly stakeholder updates. This is supported by a robust risk management strategy that uses a Risk Register to track impact and likelihood, with clear escalation paths for business-impacting blockers.

Execution and Quality Assurance (QA) are centered around a disciplined Pull Request (PR) workflow and a strict "Definition of Done." Teams are encouraged to keep PRs under 400 lines, include automated testing in CI, and require at least one approval before merging. QA practices include a mix of unit, integration, and smoke tests. Releases are standardized into Patch, Minor, and Major types, each requiring a rollback plan and post-deploy verification to minimize production risk.

To foster continuous improvement, OctoAcme mandates retrospectives after every major milestone. These sessions focus on "what went well" and "what could be improved," resulting in actionable items with assigned owners. By storing these processes in a central repository and using GitHub Issue templates for updates, the team ensures that their institutional knowledge remains a "living" resource that can be refined based on real-world feedback.
